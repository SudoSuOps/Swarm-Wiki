How to Select the Next CRE Micro-Domain

[!NOTE]
Doctrine Tags: cre micro-domain selection scoring-rubric training-strategy atlas swarm-wiki

[!TIP]
Core question:
What lane is narrow enough to win, important enough to matter, and structured enough to prove?

Summary

Not every CRE lane deserves a cook.

Some are too broad. Some are too weak commercially. Some are hard to evaluate. Some sound interesting but do not produce a real moat.

Swarm & Bee should only pursue a CRE micro-domain when the lane is:

commercially meaningful
narrow enough to define
rich enough in repeatable data
tied to a real workflow
testable with a clear proof standard
capable of compounding into defendable edge

This page defines how to choose the next lane with discipline.

Core Principle

[!IMPORTANT]
The next CRE micro-domain should not be selected by vibes, novelty, or category size.

It should be selected by:

sharpness of scope
workflow usefulness
data availability
proof potential
monetization path
compounding advantage

A good micro-domain is not just “interesting.”
It is operationally valuable and trainable.

What We Are Looking For

The best next micro-domain usually has all of the following:

1. A bounded lane

The scope is narrow and specific.

Good:

Florida Dollar General STNL ownership resolution
Texas IOS acquisition screening
bridge lender fit for multifamily deals

Bad:

retail real estate intelligence
CRE acquisitions
real estate finance assistant
2. A recurring workflow

The lane maps to a repeated job.

Examples:

resolving store → parcel → owner
generating acquisition screens
ranking lender fit
clustering repeat owners
normalizing lease abstracts
3. A known actor set

The same entities appear repeatedly.

Examples:

tenants
landlords
brokers
developers
county records
registered agents
lenders
owner LLCs
4. A record-backed evidence base

There are sources that can ground the lane in facts.

Examples:

parcel records
tax records
corporate registries
EDGAR
lease forms
offering memos
servicer reports
lender term sheets
internal deal artifacts
5. A clear commercial output

The system produces something usable.

Examples:

outreach table
ownership graph
acquisition ranking
IC memo
lender shortlist
risk summary
trade comp memo
6. A measurable proof standard

You can tell whether it works.

Examples:

owner resolution accuracy
comp precision
lender recommendation hit rate
memo completeness
extraction accuracy
risk flag precision
Selection Framework

Use the rubric below before approving any new CRE micro-domain.

Score each category from 1 to 5.

Score meanings
1 = weak
2 = below threshold
3 = usable but not exciting
4 = strong
5 = exceptional
CRE Micro-Domain Scoring Rubric
1. Scope Sharpness

Question: Is the lane truly bounded?

Score high when:

the subject is narrow
the geography is clear if relevant
the workflow is explicit
the lane can be named precisely

5 example:
Florida Dollar General STNL ownership graph intelligence

1 example:
CRE market intelligence

2. Workflow Repetition

Question: Does this lane support a job that happens over and over?

Score high when:

the task repeats frequently
the workflow is stable
outputs are predictable
process standardization is possible

5 example:
parcel-to-owner resolution for retail sites

1 example:
one-off trophy office restructuring strategy

3. Data Availability

Question: Can we gather enough material to cook and maintain the lane?

Score high when:

data is abundant
records are accessible
examples repeat
sources are structured or can be normalized

5 example:
parcel ownership + registry matching in a records-rich state

1 example:
private off-market handshake behavior with little documentation

4. Ground Truth Quality

Question: Can success be anchored to verifiable facts?

Score high when:

there are public records
pass/fail can be checked
outputs can be audited
confidence scoring is possible

5 example:
entity resolution against state registries

1 example:
“best broker instinct” with no stable source of truth

5. Commercial Value

Question: Does this lane solve a problem someone pays for?

Score high when:

it supports revenue work
it saves time on valuable workflows
it sharpens investment or outreach decisions
it improves a sellable product

5 example:
ownership intelligence for acquisition targeting

1 example:
general CRE trivia enrichment

6. Differentiation Potential

Question: Does this lane create a moat or just catch up to common knowledge?

Score high when:

the knowledge is hard to assemble
the workflow is not well served by generic models
the lane rewards specificity
the output improves with graph/data refinement

5 example:
Florida private owner clustering via parcel + entity + agent links

1 example:
generic cap-rate explanation

7. Evaluation Clarity

Question: Can we build a clean eval set for this lane?

Score high when:

pass/fail is easy to define
metrics are objective
benchmark cases are available
errors are classifiable

5 example:
lease abstract field extraction with labeled answers

1 example:
“sound more like an experienced operator”

8. Failure Loop Quality

Question: Will this lane generate useful misses that can be turned into repair pairs?

Score high when:

failures are easy to classify
bad outputs reveal specific weaknesses
repair data can be generated systematically
retraining can produce measurable gains

5 example:
owner mismatch, bad entity tieback, or wrong lease term classification

1 example:
vague strategic commentary with no precise failure type

9. Product Fit

Question: Can this lane plug into an actual Swarm & Bee product surface?

Score high when:

it fits Atlas, ownership graphs, data products, or workflow tools
it can be surfaced in UI or API
it supports a paid workflow
it helps tell a product story

5 example:
graph-powered ownership intelligence page

1 example:
isolated research lane with no delivery path

10. Compounding Potential

Question: Does work in this lane help future lanes?

Score high when:

the data model is reusable
the graph becomes richer over time
the sources support adjacent micro-domains
the lane becomes infrastructure for future products

5 example:
parcel/entity/agent resolution that later supports multiple retail brands

1 example:
one-off market color for a single niche deal type

Decision Bands

[!TIP]
Use the total score as a first filter, not the only filter.

42–50: Build now

This is a high-value micro-domain with strong training, eval, and product potential.

34–41: Strong candidate

Worth pursuing, but may need a tighter scope or better data plan.

26–33: Borderline

Interesting, but likely too broad, too weakly grounded, or too hard to monetize without refinement.

25 or below: Do not cook yet

The lane is not ready. Tighten the definition or move on.

Hard Gates

[!WARNING]
Even a decent total score should fail if any of these are true.

Do not approve the lane if:

the workflow is unclear
the proof standard is vague
the data source is too thin
the output is not commercially useful
the scope is still a category, not a lane

A lane that scores well but fails a hard gate is still not ready.

Selection Process
Step 1 — Name the lane precisely

Use the naming format:

[Geography] + [Asset / Tenant] + [Workflow] + [Output]

Example:

Florida + Dollar General STNL + ownership resolution + outreach graph
Step 2 — Identify the workflow

Ask:
What exact recurring job is this helping with?

Step 3 — Identify the actors

List the repeat players:

owners
agents
lenders
developers
registries
tenants
brokers
Step 4 — Identify the records

What sources will ground the lane?

Step 5 — Define the output

What artifact should the model or system produce?

Step 6 — Define the eval

What does success look like in measurable terms?

Step 7 — Score it

Use the rubric honestly.

Step 8 — Tighten if needed

If the score is weak, narrow the lane until it becomes trainable.

What Usually Wins

The best next CRE micro-domains usually have this shape:

specific tenant or asset
specific geography or market
concrete workflow
visible actor network
record-backed evidence
output that a real operator would use tomorrow

Examples:

Walgreens rent-bump extraction for STNL comps
Palm Beach retail owner clustering
bridge lender fit for multifamily deals
Texas IOS acquisition ranking
county parcel-to-entity resolution
AutoZone spread tracking by market
What Usually Loses

Weak candidates usually look like this:

broad category framing
unclear outputs
no benchmark set
no stable records
generic model behavior already does “okay”
too much commentary, not enough workflow

Examples:

“understands retail”
“good at acquisitions”
“finance copilot for CRE”
“knows multifamily”
Operating Rule

[!IMPORTANT]
The next micro-domain should be the lane where:

generic models fail,
records exist,
workflows repeat,
outputs matter,
and proof is possible.

That is where Swarm & Bee gets paid.

Final Doctrine

Choosing the next CRE micro-domain is not about picking the biggest category.

It is about finding the smallest lane that:

matters commercially,
can be verified clearly,
compounds into a moat,
and fits a real product surface.
