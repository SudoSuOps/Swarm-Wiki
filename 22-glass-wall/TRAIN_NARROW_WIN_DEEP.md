# Doctrine: Train Narrow, Win Deep

**Tags**: doctrine training-strategy micro-domain model-design data-refinery swarm-wiki

---

## Summary

Swarm & Bee does not build generic domain models.
Swarm & Bee builds micro-domain intelligence.

Broad domain training creates competence.
Specific domain training creates edge.

A model trained on a large category like "CRE" can learn general mechanics, language, and reasoning patterns. That is useful, but it is not enough to create a moat. Real commercial value appears when a model understands a specific lane, specific actors, specific structures, and specific market behavior.

**Train narrow. Win deep.**

---

## Core Principle

Do not train on giant categories when the value lives in subdomains.

"CRE" is too broad to be a moat.
"Florida Dollar General STNL ownership and trade intelligence" is a moat.

The goal is not to make a model vaguely familiar with an industry.
The goal is to make a model native to a trade.

That means knowing:

- the structures
- the repeat players
- the lease norms
- the ownership patterns
- the spread behavior
- the workflow reality
- the facts that insiders use and outsiders miss

---

## What We Learned

A broad CRE cook can produce a strong analyst on:

- cap rates
- debt yield
- DSCR
- underwriting logic
- capital stack reasoning
- advisory language

But it will still fail on:

- retailer-specific lease norms
- known owner/developer networks
- market-specific pricing behavior
- state-record lookup tactics
- private trade knowledge
- operator heuristics

That is the difference between:

**an analyst**

and

**an operator brain**

Swarm & Bee should optimize for operator brains.

---

## Why This Matters

### 1. Broad knowledge is easy to approximate

General domain knowledge is increasingly available everywhere. Many models can become "good enough" on broad mechanics.

That is not defendable.

### 2. Specific knowledge compounds into a moat

Micro-domain intelligence is harder to assemble, verify, and maintain. It reflects real workflow knowledge, local data, repeated edge cases, and trade-specific memory.

That is defendable.

### 3. Commercial value lives in specificity

Customers do not pay premium pricing because a model can explain a cap rate.
They pay when a model can explain why this asset, in this state, under this lease structure, owned by this entity, trades differently than the generic case.

### 4. Narrow training improves product clarity

A tightly scoped model is easier to evaluate, easier to position, easier to sell, and easier to improve. It also produces cleaner failure signals, which improves the next cook.

### 5. Defendable intelligence requires domain boundaries

When the scope is narrow, the system can be validated against real facts, real entities, real workflows, and real outputs. That makes the intelligence more auditable and more bankable.

## How We Apply It

### Layer 1: Core Mechanics

This is the foundation layer.

Teach:

- industry math
- reasoning patterns
- terminology
- structures
- underwriting logic
- baseline workflows

This creates general competence.

### Layer 2: Vertical Tradecraft

This is where the model learns a defined operating lane.

Examples:

- Dollar General STNL
- AutoZone STNL
- IOS
- medical office
- multifamily bridge
- aviation
- legal credit repair
- GEO / AI visibility

This creates domain-native behavior.

### Layer 3: Territory + Actor Intelligence

This is where the edge becomes real.

Teach:

- geography-specific spreads
- state-specific records
- owner networks
- repeat counterparties
- local workflow patterns
- market norms
- real contact / entity resolution structures
- known risk signals

This creates commercial advantage.

---

## Internal Rule

When evaluating a new cook, ask:

**Is this making the model broadly smarter, or specifically sharper?**

Broadly smarter is useful.
Specifically sharper is what wins.

---

## Build Standard

Every serious model program should define:

### 1. The lane

What exact subdomain is this model supposed to own?

### 2. The workflow

What real task does this intelligence improve?

### 3. The actors

Who are the repeat entities, operators, buyers, sellers, agents, or stakeholders?

### 4. The proof standard

How do we verify the model knows the lane rather than sounding fluent?

### 5. The failure loop

How will we capture misses, classify them, and turn them into repair pairs?

---

## Good Scopes

Strong scopes are:

- narrow
- commercial
- repeatable
- data-rich
- verifiable
- operationally relevant

Examples:

- Florida Dollar General STNL ownership graph intelligence
- AutoZone cap-rate and spread intelligence
- CRE borrower risk memo generation for bridge debt
- county-level parcel ownership resolution workflows
- aviation maintenance event classification
- credit dispute letter decisioning for FCRA / FDCPA

---

## Bad Scopes

Weak scopes are:

- too broad
- too vague
- too academic
- not tied to a workflow
- impossible to verify
- too generic to sell

Examples:

- "knows CRE"
- "understands legal"
- "good at finance"
- "helpful for healthcare"

These are categories, not moats.

**Core model**
plus
**micro-domain cooks**
plus
**validated failure loops**
plus
**workflow-specific delivery**

This is how a model stops being a demo and becomes infrastructure.

---

## Decision Filter

Before starting a new dataset or fine-tune, ask:

1. What exact subdomain are we targeting?
2. What trade knowledge separates insiders from outsiders here?
3. What facts are missing from the current cook?
4. Can we verify success with real evals?
5. Will this sharpen the model into a tool someone would pay for?

If those answers are weak, the scope is too broad.
